History & context

Magic in Skazka is a temperamental thing. It never wished to settle into flesh and steel, and so permanent enchantment is rare. Artifacts bound with long-lasting magic are few and far between—power there is, but it flees metal and wood like shadows flinch from dawn. Potions, charms, and other ephemeral magics endure, but items made to last often do not.

This fracture in the weave of magic began nearly two millennia ago. Arms and armour—once vibrant with rune and incantation—started to dull. Enchanters found their bindings slipping; metals they had strengthened with ancient rites turned brittle. The forge cracked with panic; the spell faded before the heat cooled. From that day on, no enchantment held for the ages—unless by some rare and terrible force.

This era defining moment became known as, 'The Fading'.

In the centuries that followed, the learned and the desperate alike sought answers. Scholars poured over crumbling scrolls, and enchanters risked their sanity in the forge. They turned from common iron and ashwood to rarer veins and stranger grains—substances that seemed to lie somewhere between the mundane and the arcane.

Two revelations came of their toil. The first: that some materials possessed peculiar, intrinsic qualities. Not quite magical, yet not wholly ordinary either. The second: that these same substances could cradle enchantments where iron and steel let them slip away.

But mastery came at a cost. To shape such things demanded uncommon talent, knowledge hoarded over lifetimes, and a body able to endure both heat and sorcery. The work was punishing, dangerous, and often fatal.

Among these rare mediums are whispered names: Mithril, bright and untarnishing; Darkwood, heavy with forgotten life; Soulglass, fragile yet perilous; and Voidsteel, cold as the grave. Some of these wonders cannot be mined or harvested on Skazka at all, and none are found in abundance.

Thus they are treasures beyond coin, coveted by kings, thieves, and dreamers alike.

Design Intent

Exotic materials give low‑level play a taste of wonder without collapsing balance. True magic stays rare; materials are quality modifiers, not raw bonuses.

Guiding Principles

  • Minor, situational perks.
  • No unconditional bonuses.
  • Do not stack with enchantments.

Working with exotic materials

Crafting with rare and exotic substances follows the same basic process as working with common metals like iron—but the difficulty is far greater. These materials are fragile, volatile, and demand exceptional precision. To attempt such work personally, a character must possess expertise (double proficiency) with the appropriate tools. Hiring an artisan is possible, but only a true master crafter can handle the task.

Due to the skill and risk involved, the guide price for crafting or commissioning an item from these materials is typically 15 to 20 times the normal cost.

Attempting the craft

Crafting an item from exotic materials requires an uninterrupted stretch of work that balances realism with playability. Historically, forging a suit of chain mail could take around 1,000 hours—far too burdensome for an RPG. In this system, crafting time scales instead by item type and rarity, keeping the feel of painstaking workmanship without stalling the adventure.

House rules: Forging arcane items

Items forged from these extraordinary materials possess the ability to hold a magical charge permanently, making them exceedingly rare and valuable.

However, merely possessing such an item is not enough. Several conditions must be met before the crafting process can even be attempted:

  1. Knowledge of the Formula — simply owning the formula is insufficient; the crafter must truly understand it.
  2. Proper Investment — specialized equipment and material components must be acquired at significant cost.
  3. Tool Proficiency — the individual undertaking the work must be proficient with the appropriate tools.
  4. Proficiency in Arcana

Formulas

Forumulas, can be acquired in several ways:

  1. Discovering another’s work: You may come into possession of a written formula through trade, theft, inheritance, or other means. However, simply owning it is not enough—you must decipher the formula to unravel its meaning. Such research is difficult, risky, and offers no guarantee of success. The requisite time must be spent researching the formula; this is relative to the rarity.
  2. Being taught directly: When instructed by someone who already knows the craft, you may record the knowledge in your own words and symbols. Because the learning is guided, no check is required to determine whether you grasp the formula—it becomes yours without uncertainty.
  3. Extracting knowledge from an item: By disenchanting a magical item within a crucible—a place that consumes magic itself—you can observe how its enchantment was woven. From this process, you gain the insight needed to write your own formula, capturing the essence of the magic once bound to the item.

Monetary investment

Crafting any magical item—whether a lasting artifact or a single-use consumable—requires an initial investment. This represents the purchase of alchemical glassware, volatile powders, stabilizing reagents, or other specialized equipment. This expense is abstracted into an incidentals fee, the value of which is determined by the item’s rarity.

Anchoring

The act of imbuing an exotic item with magic is called Anchoring—binding the magic permanently to the material. No tool proficiency check is required at this stage, as that skill was already tested during the creation of the exotic item itself. However, proficiency in the tool is still required.

Instead, the focus falls on the enchanter: whoever is imprinting their magic must succeed on an Arcana check. The DC is determined by the rarity of the magic item being created, not the rarity of the exotic material serving as its receptacle.

The time required to Anchor magic into an item is determined by the rarity of the item being created. However, the process is always completed in a single sitting and rarely exceeds 24 uninterrupted hours.

Note: two individual can combine their talents. Two players wishing to make a magical sword can do so as long as between them they have the required skill proficiencies. For example, Erica wants to enchant a sword with flame tongue. She is proficient with Arcana but does not know how to use Smithing Tools. That's no problem because she has hired a smith. Between them they can complete the Anchoring process. The Smith can properly heat the base sword while Erica casts the enchantments.

House rules: Brewing potions

The art of brewing potions and salves is as old as Skazka itself, practiced by hedge-witches in shadowed cottages, court alchemists in candlelit towers, and wandering herbalists who gather their craft from the wild places of the world. For thousands of years, this tradition has been passed down through whispered recipes, careful experimentation, and the slow mastery of flame, glass, and leaf.

Skazka’s lands are rich with life, home to hundreds of herbs, roots, and flowers with properties both wondrous and dangerous. To those with the patience to learn their secrets, these plants become the foundation of an art that balances knowledge, instinct, and—at times—magic itself.

Potion brewing is often divided into two distinct paths:

  • Herbal Brewing – Rooted in the natural world, herbal potions are created using a Herbalism Kit. These remedies emphasize healing, restoration, and the easing of ailments. They are the craft of healers, apothecaries, and wise-folk who know how to coax life’s essence from the earth. Herbal brewing covers any potion which cures, heals, neutralises or removes an effect.
  • Magical Brewing – More complex and volatile, magical potions require Alchemist’s Supplies. These concoctions may grant temporary powers, alter the body, or channel arcane energies into liquid form. The work of alchemists and enchanters, such brews are as perilous as they are potent.

Though not costly in materials, brewing demands dedication. Every potion requires time, patience, and effort—along with the mastery of a specific formula. Without such knowledge, even the simplest mixture is little more than flavored water. With it, however, the brewer can transform common herbs and arcane reagents into draughts capable of mending wounds, shielding the soul, or altering fate itself. The practiioner must also be proficient in the relevant tools.

The rules and requirements for learning forumulas is the same as those set out above for Forging Arcane items. Formulas for potions can be found here. Once you have deciphered or created your own formula you may brew that potion provided you have the relevant tools, ingredients and time.

To summarise:

  • You must be proficient with a Herbalism Kit or Alchemist's Supplies.
  • You must have immediate access to those tools.
  • You must know the formula for the potion you wish to make.
  • You must have the ingredients set out in the formula.
  • You must have a potion bottle, vial or flask in which to decant your finished concoction
  • Note: You do not need to pay an incidental cost for potion brewing like you must for crafting arcane items.
  • You must spend the required amount of time relatively uninterrupted. At the end, and only at the end, of the required time the potion is ready. For example, you can brew a potion of healing during a long rest. That does not stop you from taking a long rest; it assumes you begin the process and leave it brewing for the duration. If you were to be attacked during that time, your DM must decide what risk there is to the process or end product.
  • No skill check is required.

Scribing magical scrolls

To the untrained eye, a wizard’s scroll might look like nothing more than elegant script on crisp vellum. But in truth, each scroll is the product of ritual, discipline, and rare materials — a conduit by which a spell is bound into physical form. In Skazka, the process of scribing a magical scroll is treated as a specialized craft, one distinct from simply “writing” — it is weaving magic.

The key ingredients for creation of magical scrolls are arcane ink and special parchments. Ordinary ink and paper will not suffice — they cannot hold the magical geometries or resist the energy infusion required.

The inks and parchments are graded by rarity tiers (common, uncommon, rare, very rare, legendary, etc.), and higher-level spells demand more exquisite grades.

A scribe must purchase or procure these materials before scribing begins; rare inks or parchments may be found only in wizard’s guilds, arcane libraries, or lost ruins.

To inscribe a spell, the scribe must already know or be able to cast that spell (i.e. it must be among their prepared spells or known spell repertoire). Without that knowledge, the mystical binding fails (the scroll won’t “take”). The sage must also be proficient in Arcana.

As the scribe works, they channel their own magical insight, working the glyphs and infusing the arcane ink with the spell’s pattern, until it becomes a magical scroll.

To summarise:

  • You must be proficient in Arcana
  • You must have the spell you wish to scribe prepared.
  • You must have the ingredients set out in the formula: Arcane Ink and parchment.
  • You must have the material components of the spell you wish to scribe.
  • Note: You do not need to pay an incidental cost for scribing scrolls like you must for crafting arcane items.
  • You must spend the required amount of time relatively uninterrupted. At the end, and only at the end, of the required time is the scroll ready.
  • No skill check is required.

Other consumable magic items

Scrolls and potions are not the only consumable magic items that can be enchanted. Dusts, oils, ammunition and a range of other magical items are considered consumable; that is to say thir hold on magic is fleeting and fragile. They will not last forever. Crafting these items follows the rules for that items type, with one exception: a successful skill check is never required.
